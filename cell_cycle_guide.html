01 — Overview

Cell cycle phases

The eukaryotic cell cycle is divided into interphase (G1, S, G2) and mitosis (M phase). Cells may also exit into a quiescent state called G0. Each transition is governed by cyclin/CDK complexes and monitored by checkpoints.

02 — Checkpoint

G1/S checkpoint

The G1/S checkpoint (also called the restriction point) is the most clinically important cell cycle checkpoint. The cell evaluates DNA integrity and extracellular growth signals. Passing this point commits the cell irreversibly to division.

Key concept: the restriction point

Before the restriction point, removing growth factors allows the cell to retreat to G0. After it, the CDK2/E2F positive feedback loop is self-sustaining — the cell divides regardless of external signals. This is the threshold that cancer cells bypass.

Ras is upstream, not immediate

This is why Ras-GDP binding (choice B in the original question) is wrong — it precedes Rb phosphorylation by multiple steps. The question asks for the most immediate predecessor to S phase entry, which is Rb phosphorylation by the cyclin/CDK complexes.

04 — Molecular machinery

Cyclin D / CDK4

The Cyclin D / CDK4 complex is the first kinase complex activated in G1. CDK4 (and CDK6) requires Cyclin D binding for activation. CDK4 alone is catalytically inert.

Function

Partial Rb phosphorylation
Mono-phosphorylates Rb at specific sites — loosens but does not release E2F
›
p27 sequestration
Cyclin D binds and sequesters p27, freeing CDK2 to become active
›

Unique property: growth factor sensor

Unlike Cyclin E, A, B (which oscillate with the intrinsic cell clock), Cyclin D is almost entirely controlled by extracellular signals. Withdraw growth factors → Cyclin D mRNA decays in minutes → complex dissolves → CDK4 goes quiet → cell retreats to G0.

05 — Molecular machinery

Cyclin E / CDK2

Cyclin E/CDK2 delivers the decisive blow to Rb at the late G1/S boundary. It hyperphosphorylates Rb at the remaining sites that CDK4 could not reach, fully releasing E2F. This creates a positive feedback loop that is self-amplifying past the restriction point.

06 — Molecular machinery

Retinoblastoma protein (Rb)

Rb is the master regulator of the G1/S transition — the "guardian of the checkpoint." It has two functional states controlled by phosphorylation status.

Active Rb (dephosphorylated)
Binds E2F. Recruits HDAC. Compacts chromatin. Prevents S phase. Found in G0, early G1.
›
Inactive Rb (phosphorylated)
Cannot bind E2F. E2F free to activate S-phase genes. Remains phosphorylated until mitosis ends.
›

Rb as a tumor suppressor — Knudson's two-hit hypothesis

Retinoblastoma (childhood eye tumor) arises when both copies of the RB1 gene are inactivated. Hereditary cases carry one germline mutation — only one somatic "hit" is needed. Sporadic cases require two independent somatic mutations in the same cell (rare). Loss of Rb also seen in osteosarcoma, small cell lung cancer, bladder cancer.

Viral proteins that mimic Rb phosphorylation

Several oncogenic viruses encode proteins that directly bind and inactivate Rb, bypassing the need for CDK-mediated phosphorylation:

08 — CDK inhibitors

p27 — the cell cycle brake pedal

p27 (encoded by CDKN1B) is a CIP/KIP family CDK inhibitor. It physically occupies the active site of Cyclin E/CDK2, preventing kinase activity and stalling G1→S progression.

p27 in cancer — low expression = poor prognosis

p27 mutations are rare, but cancers reduce p27 protein by overexpressing Skp2 (accelerating degradation) or mislocalizing p27 to the cytoplasm where it cannot inhibit nuclear CDK2. Low nuclear p27 is a prognostic marker in breast, colon, gastric, prostate, and many other cancers.

09 — CDK inhibitors

p21 & the p53 axis

p21 (CDKN1A) is the transcriptional target of p53 — it bridges DNA damage sensing and cell cycle arrest. When DNA damage is detected, p53 is stabilized and drives p21 expression, halting the cycle at G1/S.

p53 vs p27: know the difference

p27 responds to growth factor withdrawal and contact inhibition — it's about environmental signals. p21 responds to DNA damage via p53 — it's about genomic integrity. Both inhibit CDK2, but via completely different upstream triggers.

10 — Clinical correlates

Cancer mechanisms at the G1/S checkpoint

Virtually every cancer has found at least one way to bypass the G1/S checkpoint. The most common strategies target the Rb/E2F axis directly.

11 — Pharmacology

CDK4/6 inhibitors

CDK4/6 inhibitors block the first step of Rb phosphorylation, locking tumor cells in G1. They are now standard of care in hormone receptor-positive (HR+), HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer, and are being investigated in many other Rb-intact tumors.

Palbociclib (Ibrance)
First-in-class CDK4/6 inhibitor · FDA approved 2015 · HR+/HER2− breast cancer
Ribociclib (Kisqali)
CDK4/6 inhibitor · OS benefit shown in premenopausal women · HR+/HER2− breast cancer
Abemaciclib (Verzenio)
CDK4/6 inhibitor · Also approved in early-stage high-risk HR+/HER2− breast cancer · More selective for CDK4

Biomarker: Rb expression required

CDK4/6 inhibitors only work if the tumor has intact Rb — if Rb is deleted (e.g. SCLC), there is nothing for phosphorylated CDK4 to act on, and the drug has no effect. This is why SCLC (which almost universally loses Rb) does not respond to these agents.

14 — Practice

Practice questions

1. A 4-year-old presents with leukocoria (white pupillary reflex) in one eye. Genetic testing reveals a germline mutation in one copy of the RB1 gene. Which additional event is most likely required for tumor formation?
Correct. This is Knudson's two-hit hypothesis. One germline RB1 mutation is inherited. The tumor forms only when the second allele is lost somatically in a retinal cell (second "hit"). Options A, B, and E would leave Rb intact — just unable to be phosphorylated or stabilized — but functional Rb protein from the wild-type allele would still suppress the cycle.
Incorrect. Knudson's two-hit hypothesis: the hereditary RB1 mutation provides the first hit, but tumor formation requires loss of the second wild-type copy in a somatic retinal cell. A, B, and E affect upstream regulators of Rb phosphorylation but don't eliminate Rb protein itself.
2. A cancer researcher treats a breast cancer cell line with a CDK4/6 inhibitor. Flow cytometry shows cell cycle arrest. In which phase would you expect the cells to accumulate?
Correct. CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib) block the initial Rb phosphorylation step. Without phosphorylated Rb, E2F stays sequestered and S-phase genes are not transcribed. Cells cannot cross the restriction point and accumulate in G1.
Incorrect. CDK4/6 is active in G1. Blocking it prevents Rb phosphorylation, trapping E2F and halting progression through the G1/S checkpoint. Cells accumulate in G1.
3. TGF-β is found to arrest cells in G1 via upregulation of p15 (INK4b). Which downstream event is most directly responsible for this arrest?
Correct. p15 is an INK4 family inhibitor that blocks CDK4/6. With CDK4/6 inhibited, Rb cannot be phosphorylated and remains in its active (dephosphorylated) state, keeping E2F transcription factors bound and inactive. S-phase gene programs are not initiated.
Incorrect. p15 inhibits CDK4/6, which are required to initiate Rb phosphorylation. Unphosphorylated Rb remains active, binds E2F, and prevents transcription of S-phase genes. This is the direct G1 arrest mechanism. Cyclin B/CDK1 and caspases are not part of the G1/S checkpoint.
