Enzyme Kinetics

Michaelis-Menten · Lineweaver-Burk · Inhibition

Michaelis-Menten Equation

v = (Vmax × [S]) / (Km + [S])
  • Curve shape: rectangular hyperbola
  • Vmax — maximum velocity when all active sites saturated
  • Km — [S] at which v = ½Vmax; reflects enzyme–substrate affinity
  • Low Km = high affinity; High Km = low affinity
  • When [S] ≫ Km: zero-order kinetics (rate independent of [S])
  • When [S] ≪ Km: first-order kinetics (rate ∝ [S])
  • Catalytic efficiency = kcat / Km

Lineweaver-Burk Plot (Double Reciprocal)

1/v = (Km/Vmax)(1/[S]) + 1/Vmax
Y-intercept = 1/Vmax  |  X-intercept = −1/Km  |  Slope = Km/Vmax

Key Points per Type

  • Competitive: overcome by ↑[S]; Km ↑, Vmax same. Examples: statins (HMG-CoA reductase), methotrexate (DHFR)
  • Non-competitive: cannot overcome with ↑[S]; Vmax ↓, Km unchanged. Example: lead on δ-ALA dehydratase
  • Uncompetitive: Both Km and Vmax decrease → apparent affinity increases
  • Irreversible: covalent modification; new enzyme must be synthesized. Examples: aspirin→COX, penicillin→transpeptidase, organophosphates→AChE, arsenic→lipoic acid
Mnemonic: Competitive inhibition → Changes Km (↑), Vmax same. You Can Compete with more substrate.

Other Regulatory Mechanisms

  • Allosteric regulation: molecule binds non-active site → conformational change → activate or inhibit
  • Covalent modification: phosphorylation (kinases) / dephosphorylation (phosphatases)
  • Feedback inhibition: end product inhibits earlier enzyme in pathway
  • Zymogen activation: inactive precursor cleaved → active form (trypsinogen→trypsin, pepsinogen→pepsin)
Clinical Pearl: Statins competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase — rate-limiting step of cholesterol synthesis. Even at high statin doses, sufficient substrate can partially overcome inhibition.

Gluconeogenesis

Liver (main) + Kidney · Triggered by hypoglycemia, fasting, cortisol

Formation of new glucose from non-carbohydrate sources. Uses 7 reversible glycolytic enzymes + 4 unique bypass enzymes.

Key rule: Acetyl-CoA CANNOT contribute net carbons to glucose (both carbons lost as CO₂ in Krebs). Even-chain fatty acids cannot be gluconeogenic substrates.

Substrates & Entry Points

Lactate (Cori Cycle)

  • Lactate → Pyruvate (LDH, cytosol)
  • Pyruvate → OAA (pyruvate carboxylase, mitochondria, requires biotin + ATP + CO₂)
  • OAA can't cross inner mito membrane → converted to Malate → crosses → back to OAA in cytosol
  • OAA → PEP (PEPCK, requires GTP)

Glycerol

  • From triglyceride lipolysis (fat breakdown)
  • Glycerol → Glycerol-3-phosphate (glycerol kinase) → DHAP → enters pathway

Glucogenic Amino Acids

  • Transamination → keto acids → enter as pyruvate or Krebs intermediates
  • Alanine → Pyruvate (ALT, needs PLP/B6) → most important gluconeogenic AA
  • Glutamine/Glutamate → α-KG (enters Krebs → OAA → PEP)
  • Only Leucine & Lysine are purely ketogenic (cannot contribute to glucose)

Odd-Chain Fatty Acids

  • Last β-oxidation round → Propionyl-CoA (3C)
  • Propionyl-CoA → Methylmalonyl-CoA (propionyl-CoA carboxylase, biotin)
  • Methylmalonyl-CoA → Succinyl-CoA (methylmalonyl-CoA mutase, Vit B12) → Krebs → gluconeogenesis

Pentose Phosphate Pathway

Location: Cytosol · Also called: HMP Shunt

Produces NADPH (reducing power) and Ribose-5-phosphate (for nucleotides). No ATP produced directly.

Oxidative Phase (Irreversible) — 2 NADPH produced

StepReactionEnzymeCofactor
1G6P → 6-phosphogluconolactoneG6P dehydrogenase (rate-limiting)NADP⁺ → NADPH
26-phosphogluconolactone → 6-phosphogluconateLactonaseH₂O

Net per G6P: 2 NADPH + 1 Ribulose-5-P + 1 CO₂

Non-Oxidative Phase (Reversible)

  • Ribulose-5-P → Ribose-5-P (isomerase) — nucleotide synthesis
  • Interconverts C3–C7 sugar phosphates via transketolase (needs Thiamine/B1) and transaldolase
  • Products (F6P, G3P) re-enter glycolysis
  • Allows nucleotide synthesis even without the oxidative phase (when NADPH is not needed)

NADPH Uses

  • Fatty acid synthesis (FAS complex needs NADPH)
  • Cholesterol synthesis
  • Glutathione reduction: GSSG → GSH (protects RBCs from oxidative damage)
  • Cytochrome P450 (drug/steroid metabolism)
  • Respiratory burst: NADPH oxidase → superoxide (O₂•⁻) for phagocytes
  • Tyrosine hydroxylase (catecholamine synthesis)

Regulation

  • G6P dehydrogenase is the rate-limiting enzyme
  • Activated by: ↑ NADP⁺ (product of NADPH oxidation), insulin
  • Inhibited by: ↑ NADPH (product inhibition)
  • Most active in: liver, adrenal cortex, RBCs, lactating mammary gland, gonads
Clinical Pearl: G6PD deficiency — most common human enzyme defect; X-linked recessive. ↓ NADPH → ↓ GSH → RBCs lyse under oxidative stress. Triggers: fava beans, dapsone, primaquine, nitrofurantoin, infections. Blood smear: Heinz bodies (denatured Hb), bite cells. Protective against malaria.
Mnemonic: G6PD deficiency triggers: DANG — Dapsone, Antivirals/Anti-malarials (primaquine), Nitrofurantoin, Gluten (fava beans)

PDH Deficiency

  • Most common: E1 (TPP-binding) subunit mutation — X-linked
  • → Lactic acidosis (pyruvate → lactate instead of Krebs)
  • Neurologic symptoms: cannot make acetyl-CoA for brain
  • Treatment: high-fat low-carb diet + thiamine supplementation

Clinical Pearl: Arsenic poisoning inhibits lipoic acid (E2 cofactor) → blocks both PDH and α-KG DH. Signs: Mees' lines, peripheral neuropathy, garlic breath.

Chemiosmotic Theory (Peter Mitchell)

  • Electrons flow through ETC → energy released → pumps H⁺ from matrix to IMS
  • Electrochemical gradient (proton motive force): ΔΨ (electrical) + ΔpH
  • H⁺ flows back through ATP synthase F₀ channel → rotates F₁ → ADP + Pi → ATP
  • ~3 H⁺ per ATP synthesized

Uncouplers

  • Dissipate H⁺ gradient without making ATP → energy released as heat
  • 2,4-DNP: historical weight-loss drug; proton ionophore
  • Thermogenin (UCP-1): brown adipose tissue; physiologic uncoupling for thermogenesis in neonates
  • Uncouplers → ↑ O₂ consumption, ↑ heat, ↓ ATP, hyperthermia
Clinical Pearl: Cyanide (CN⁻), CO, and H₂S all inhibit Complex IV. CN⁻ antidote: hydroxocobalamin (binds CN⁻) or nitrites (form methemoglobin, which attracts CN⁻ away from Complex IV) + thiosulfate (converts CN⁻→thiocyanate).
Clinical Pearl: MPTP → Complex I inhibitor → destroys dopaminergic neurons → parkinsonism. Used to create animal Parkinson models.

ATP Yield — Palmitate (16C) Example

  • 8 Acetyl-CoA from 7 rounds → 8 × Krebs cycle
  • 7 rounds β-oxidation → 7 FADH₂ + 7 NADH
  • 8 Acetyl-CoA × (3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 GTP) = 24 NADH + 8 FADH₂ + 8 GTP
  • Total NADH: 7+24 = 31 × 2.5 = 77.5; FADH₂: 7+8 = 15 × 1.5 = 22.5; GTP: 8
  • − 2 ATP activation = ~106 ATP net (using modern P/O ratios)

Special Cases of Beta-Oxidation

Odd-Chain Fatty Acids

  • Final product: Propionyl-CoA (3C) instead of Acetyl-CoA
  • Propionyl-CoA → Methylmalonyl-CoA (propionyl-CoA carboxylase, biotin)
  • Methylmalonyl-CoA → Succinyl-CoA (methylmalonyl-CoA mutase, Vit B12) → Krebs
Clinical Pearl: Vit B12 deficiency → methylmalonyl-CoA accumulates → methylmalonic acidemia. Also causes megaloblastic anemia and subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord.

Unsaturated Fatty Acids

  • Cis double bonds require extra enzymes: isomerase (monounsaturated) or reductase + isomerase (polyunsaturated)
  • Yield slightly less ATP (one fewer FADH₂ per pre-existing double bond)

Peroxisomal Beta-Oxidation (Very Long Chain FA, >22C)

  • First step uses O₂ → H₂O₂ (not FAD→FADH₂) — energy released as heat, NOT as FADH₂
  • H₂O₂ neutralized by catalase
  • Defect: Zellweger syndrome (absent peroxisomes) → accumulation of VLCFAs

Key Steps of FA Synthesis

  • Acetyl-CoA → Malonyl-CoA: Acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC), rate-limiting, needs biotin + ATP
  • FAS (Fatty Acid Synthase) complex: adds 2C at a time using malonyl-CoA and NADPH
  • 16 rounds → Palmitate (16C) — most common product
  • Requires 7 Malonyl-CoA + 1 Acetyl-CoA + 14 NADPH per palmitate

Triglyceride Synthesis

  • Glycerol-3-phosphate + 3 Fatty Acyl-CoA → Triglyceride (via sequential esterification)
  • Occurs in liver and adipose tissue
  • Liver packages into VLDL for export

Triglyceride Mobilization (Lipolysis)

  • Adipose tissue breaks TAG → Glycerol + 3 FFAs (free fatty acids)
  • Stimulated by: glucagon, epinephrine, cortisol, growth hormone, sympathetic NS
  • Inhibited by: insulin
  • Enzymes activated by phosphorylation:
  •   • Adipose triglyceride lipase (ATGL): TAG → DAG
  •   • Hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL): DAG → MAG (rate-limiting, most regulated)
  •   • Monoglyceride lipase: MAG → Glycerol + FFA
  • Glycerol → liver → gluconeogenesis
  • FFAs → blood bound to albumin → tissues for β-oxidation

Q-Bank

USMLE-Style MCQs — All Topics

Q1  ·  EK
A drug blocks an enzyme by binding irreversibly to its active site. Kinetic analysis shows Vmax is decreased. Addition of excess substrate does NOT restore activity. Which mechanism best describes this inhibitor?
Answer: D
Irreversible inhibitors form covalent bonds with the active site. Vmax ↓ and cannot be overcome by adding more substrate. Competitive inhibitors can be overcome by ↑[S]. Noncompetitive/uncompetitive are reversible.
Q2  ·  EK
On a Lineweaver-Burk plot, an inhibitor shifts the line to give the same Y-intercept but a different (more negative) X-intercept. Which type of inhibition is this?
Answer: A
Same Y-intercept = same Vmax. Different X-intercept = different Km (↑ Km, apparent). This is competitive inhibition. Noncompetitive: same X-intercept, different Y-intercept. Uncompetitive: parallel lines.
Q3  ·  EK
An enzyme has a Km of 2 mM for its substrate. A researcher runs the reaction at a substrate concentration of 0.2 mM. What best describes the kinetics?
Answer: B
When [S] ≪ Km (0.2 mM ≪ 2 mM), the enzyme operates in first-order kinetics — rate is proportional to [S]. Zero-order applies when [S] ≫ Km (enzyme saturated).
Q4  ·  EK
Which enzyme inhibitor is best characterized as uncompetitive?
Answer: B
Uncompetitive inhibitors bind only the ES complex → both Vmax and Km decrease by the same factor → parallel lines on Lineweaver-Burk. Apparent Km ↓ means apparent affinity ↑.
Q5  ·  GLYC
Which enzyme catalyzes the rate-limiting, committed step of glycolysis and is allosterically activated by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate?
Answer: C
PFK-1 catalyzes the conversion of F6P → F1,6-BP. It is the rate-limiting, committed step of glycolysis. Activated by AMP, ADP, F-2,6-BP (most potent). Inhibited by ATP, citrate.
Q6  ·  GLYC
A patient with a genetic disorder lacks GLUT4 in muscle and adipose tissue. Which hormone would be most impaired in its ability to stimulate glucose uptake?
Answer: C
GLUT4 is the insulin-sensitive glucose transporter in muscle and adipose. Insulin stimulates GLUT4 translocation to the cell membrane. Without GLUT4, insulin cannot promote glucose uptake in these tissues.
Q7  ·  GLYC
During anaerobic glycolysis, pyruvate is converted to lactate by lactate dehydrogenase. What is the metabolic purpose of this reaction?
Answer: B
The conversion of pyruvate → lactate oxidizes NADH → NAD⁺. This regenerates NAD⁺ needed for step 6 of glycolysis (G3P dehydrogenase), allowing glycolysis to continue under anaerobic conditions. No net NADH is produced in anaerobic glycolysis.
Q8  ·  GLYC
A 22-year-old athlete has normal resting lactate levels but markedly elevated lactate during intense sprinting. Which enzyme is responsible for converting glucose to G6P in his muscle?
Answer: B
Muscle uses hexokinase (HK I–III), not glucokinase. Hexokinase has high affinity (low Km) for glucose, is present in most tissues, and is inhibited by G6P. Glucokinase is liver/β-cell specific.
Q9  ·  GLYC
Glucokinase differs from hexokinase in that glucokinase: (select the CORRECT statement)
Answer: C
Glucokinase is induced by insulin. It has HIGH Km (low affinity), is NOT inhibited by G6P (unlike hexokinase), and is found only in hepatocytes and pancreatic β-cells.
Q10  ·  GLUCO
Which of the following is the rate-limiting step in gluconeogenesis from pyruvate, and what cofactor does it require?
Answer: B
Pyruvate carboxylase converts pyruvate → OAA (first committed step from pyruvate). It requires biotin (CO₂ carrier) and ATP. PEPCK then converts OAA → PEP (requires GTP) — a critical but downstream step.
Q11  ·  GLUCO
A fasting patient's serum cortisol is markedly elevated. Which of the following best explains how cortisol promotes gluconeogenesis?
Answer: B
Cortisol is the prime gluconeogenic hormone. It promotes protein breakdown → amino acids (especially alanine) → gluconeogenesis. It also increases expression of PEPCK and other gluconeogenic enzymes. Insulin inhibits gluconeogenesis; AMPK inhibits it.
Q12  ·  GLUCO
Which enzyme is uniquely required to release free glucose from the liver and is absent in muscle?
Answer: C
Glucose-6-phosphatase (located in smooth ER) converts G6P → free glucose, which can exit hepatocytes. Absent in muscle and brain, which is why they cannot release free glucose into the bloodstream.
Q13  ·  PPP
A 35-year-old male of African descent develops hemolytic anemia after being prescribed primaquine for malaria prophylaxis. His peripheral smear shows Heinz bodies. Which enzyme deficiency best explains this presentation?
Answer: C
G6PD deficiency is X-linked and common in Africans and Mediterranean populations. ↓ G6PD → ↓ NADPH → ↓ GSH → RBCs susceptible to oxidative damage. Primaquine/dapsone/fava beans/infection precipitate hemolysis. Heinz bodies = denatured Hb.
Q14  ·  PPP
Which cofactor does transketolase require, and what is the clinical consequence of its deficiency?
Answer: C
Transketolase (non-oxidative phase of PPP) requires thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP). Deficiency → Wernicke's encephalopathy (confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia). Erythrocyte transketolase activity is used to diagnose thiamine deficiency.
Q15  ·  PPP
NADPH produced by the pentose phosphate pathway is LEAST important for which of the following?
Answer: D
Substrate-level phosphorylation (in glycolysis and Krebs) uses ATP/GTP directly and does not involve NADPH. NADPH is critical for FA synthesis, respiratory burst (NADPH oxidase), and maintaining reduced glutathione (GSH) in RBCs.
Q16  ·  GLYCOGEN
A 5-year-old presents with severe fasting hypoglycemia, massive hepatomegaly, elevated lactate, and high triglycerides. Muscle strength is normal. Which enzyme is most likely deficient?
Answer: C
Von Gierke disease (Type I GSD): glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency. Cannot release glucose from G6P → severe hypoglycemia; G6P → glycolysis → ↑ pyruvate → ↑ lactate; G6P also → FA synthesis → ↑ TG. No muscle involvement. Liver and kidney affected.
Q17  ·  GLYCOGEN
Which of the following correctly describes the regulation of glycogen synthase?
Answer: B
Glycogen synthase is ACTIVE when dephosphorylated. Insulin → protein phosphatase-1 → dephosphorylates (activates) glycogen synthase. Glucagon/epinephrine → PKA → phosphorylates glycogen synthase → INACTIVE.
Q18  ·  GLYCOGEN
An infant presents with hypotonia, cardiomegaly, and respiratory failure. Echocardiography shows hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. Muscle biopsy shows glycogen accumulation within lysosomes. Which disease is this?
Answer: C
Pompe disease: lysosomal acid maltase (α-1,4-glucosidase) deficiency → glycogen accumulates in lysosomes → cardiomegaly, hypotonia. Infantile form is rapidly fatal without treatment (alglucosidase alfa — enzyme replacement therapy).
Q19  ·  TRANS
A patient with PDH complex deficiency presents with lactic acidosis and neurological impairment. Which of the following cofactors, if deficient, would directly impair the E1 subunit of PDH?
Answer: B
E1 (pyruvate decarboxylase) requires thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP). Thiamine deficiency → E1 dysfunction → pyruvate cannot be converted to acetyl-CoA → accumulates as lactate → lactic acidosis.
Q20  ·  TRANS
In which condition would pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase (PDK) be MOST active?
Answer: B
PDK is activated by high Acetyl-CoA and high NADH (products of PDH) → phosphorylates PDH → turns it OFF. This prevents excessive pyruvate oxidation when the cell has enough energy. Think: fed/resting state → products activate PDK.
Q21  ·  KREBS
Which Krebs cycle enzyme is inhibited by fluorocitrate (formed from fluoroacetate, rat poison)?
Answer: B
Fluoroacetate → fluoroacetyl-CoA → fluorocitrate (via citrate synthase) → competitively inhibits aconitase (Step 2). This is 'lethal synthesis' — the precursor itself is not toxic; its metabolite is.
Q22  ·  KREBS
Which of the following mutations is correctly matched to its associated tumor?
Answer: B
FH (fumarase) mutations cause hereditary leiomyomatosis and renal cell carcinoma (HLRCC). SDH mutations cause pheochromocytoma/paraganglioma. IDH1/2 mutations cause glioma, AML, cholangiocarcinoma.
Q23  ·  KREBS
A low GTP/GDP ratio would be expected to have what effect on the Krebs cycle?
Answer: B
Low GTP = low energy → stimulates succinyl-CoA synthetase (Step 5) to convert succinyl-CoA → succinate + GTP. High ATP (not GTP) inhibits citrate synthase and isocitrate DH. GTP/GDP ratio directly affects Step 5.
Q24  ·  ETC
Cyanide poisoning prevents cellular respiration by inhibiting which complex of the electron transport chain?
Answer: D
Cyanide (CN⁻), CO, and azide inhibit Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) by binding to the Fe³⁺ of cytochrome a₃, preventing electron transfer to O₂. Treatment: hydroxocobalamin or nitrites + thiosulfate.
Q25  ·  ETC
Which of the following is the correct effect of 2,4-dinitrophenol (2,4-DNP)?
Answer: C
2,4-DNP is an uncoupler — it carries H⁺ across the inner mitochondrial membrane (bypassing ATP synthase), dissipating the proton gradient. This ↑ O₂ consumption and ↑ heat production, but ↓ ATP synthesis. Used historically as a weight loss drug.
Q26  ·  ETC
The malate-aspartate shuttle is preferred over the glycerophosphate shuttle in the liver because:
Answer: B
Malate-aspartate shuttle transfers cytosolic NADH equivalents into the mitochondria as NADH → Complex I → 2.5 ATP per NADH. Glycerophosphate shuttle enters as FADH₂ → Complex II → only 1.5 ATP. MAS is more energy-efficient.
Q27  ·  FA
Which enzyme is the rate-limiting step of fatty acid beta-oxidation in the mitochondria, and what inhibits it?
Answer: B
CPT-1 (carnitine palmitoyl transferase 1) is the rate-limiting step of fatty acid transport into mitochondria (required for β-oxidation). Malonyl-CoA (the FA synthesis intermediate) inhibits CPT-1 — preventing futile cycling of synthesis and oxidation simultaneously.
Q28  ·  FA
A 3-year-old presents with fasting hypoglycemia, hypoketosis, and elevated medium-chain acylcarnitines. Which enzyme deficiency is most likely?
Answer: B
MCAD deficiency: cannot oxidize medium-chain FA → ↓ acetyl-CoA → ↓ ketogenesis → hypoketotic hypoglycemia. Elevated medium-chain acylcarnitines on newborn screen. Most common FA oxidation disorder. Fatal if undetected (SIDS).
Q29  ·  FA
In patients with odd-chain fatty acid oxidation, what vitamin is required to convert methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA?
Answer: C
Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase requires adenosylcobalamin (Vit B12). Deficiency → methylmalonyl-CoA accumulates → methylmalonic acidemia. Also causes megaloblastic anemia. Propionyl-CoA carboxylase (prior step) requires biotin.
Q30  ·  FA
A patient on a prolonged fast develops ketoacidosis. In which organ does ketogenesis primarily occur, and which enzyme is rate-limiting?
Answer: A
Ketogenesis occurs in hepatic mitochondria. Rate-limiting enzyme is mitochondrial HMG-CoA synthase. The liver synthesizes ketones but CANNOT use them (lacks succinyl-CoA transferase/thiophorase).
Q31  ·  FA
What is the primary regulatory mechanism that prevents simultaneous fatty acid synthesis and beta-oxidation?
Answer: B
When FA synthesis is active (↑ malonyl-CoA), CPT-1 is inhibited → FA cannot enter mitochondria → no β-oxidation. This is the classic reciprocal regulation preventing futile cycling.
Q32  ·  LIPID
A patient has a mutation in ApoC-II. Which of the following would you expect?
Answer: B
ApoC-II activates lipoprotein lipase (LPL) on capillary endothelium. Without ApoC-II, LPL cannot degrade TG in chylomicrons/VLDL → massive hypertriglyceridemia → pancreatitis, eruptive xanthomas.
Q33  ·  LIPID
Statins lower plasma LDL by which primary mechanism?
Answer: B
Statins competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase → ↓ hepatic cholesterol synthesis → ↓ intracellular cholesterol → upregulation of LDL receptors → ↑ clearance of LDL from plasma.
Q34  ·  LIPID
Which lipoprotein is responsible for reverse cholesterol transport, and which apolipoprotein activates the key enzyme in this process?
Answer: B
HDL performs reverse cholesterol transport. ApoA-I activates LCAT (lecithin-cholesterol acyltransferase), which esterifies free cholesterol on HDL. This allows HDL to accumulate cholesterol and return it to the liver.
Q35  ·  AA
A 6-month-old boy presents with hyperammonemia, orotic aciduria, and lethargy. Which enzyme deficiency is most likely?
Answer: B
OTC deficiency is the most common urea cycle defect and is X-linked. Carbamoyl phosphate (CP) accumulates → spills into cytosol → enters pyrimidine synthesis → ↑ orotic acid. CPS-1 deficiency also causes ↑ NH₃ but NO orotic acid (CP is not made).
Q36  ·  AA
A patient with elevated serum AST and ALT has hepatocellular damage. Both enzymes require which cofactor?
Answer: C
AST and ALT are transaminases — all transaminases require PLP (the active form of Vitamin B6) as cofactor. They transfer amino groups to α-ketoglutarate, forming glutamate.
Q37  ·  AA
N-acetylglutamate (NAG) is essential for urea cycle function because:
Answer: B
N-acetylglutamate is an obligate allosteric activator of CPS-1 (carbamoyl phosphate synthetase I). Without NAG, CPS-1 is inactive and the urea cycle cannot function. NAG is synthesized from glutamate + acetyl-CoA by NAG synthase (activated by arginine).
Q38  ·  AA
Ammonia toxicity in the brain is primarily mediated by which mechanism?
Answer: C
In astrocytes, excess NH₄⁺ is incorporated into glutamate → glutamine (by glutamine synthetase). Glutamine is osmotically active → draws water → astrocyte swelling → cerebral edema → ↑ ICP → herniation. α-KG also depleted → ↓ Krebs/ATP.
Q39  ·  NUC
A child presents with severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID), lymphopenia, and elevated deoxyadenosine in urine. Which enzyme deficiency is responsible?
Answer: B
ADA deficiency → deoxyadenosine accumulates → phosphorylated to dATP → dATP inhibits ribonucleotide reductase → T and B cells cannot make dNTPs for DNA replication → SCID. First disease treated by gene therapy.
Q40  ·  NUC
5-Fluorouracil (5-FU) exerts its anti-cancer effect primarily by:
Answer: B
5-FU → 5-F-dUMP → suicide inhibitor of thymidylate synthase (needs THF as cofactor). This prevents conversion of dUMP → dTMP → ↓ dTTP → ↓ DNA synthesis. Methotrexate/trimethoprim inhibit DHFR (blocks THF regeneration).
Q41  ·  NUC
Which of the following correctly distinguishes de novo purine synthesis from pyrimidine synthesis?
Answer: A
In purine synthesis, the ring is assembled step-by-step directly on PRPP (ribose-first). In pyrimidine synthesis, the pyrimidine ring (via OMP) is fully assembled first, then attached to PRPP. Key distinguishing feature for boards.
Q42  ·  NUC
Lesch-Nyhan syndrome results from HGPRT deficiency. Which metabolic consequence best explains the hyperuricemia?
Answer: A,B
Without HGPRT, free purines (hypoxanthine, guanine) cannot be salvaged → PRPP accumulates → ↑ de novo purine synthesis → ↑ purine degradation → ↑ uric acid. Also, purines are directly catabolized to uric acid via xanthine oxidase.
